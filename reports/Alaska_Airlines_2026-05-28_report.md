# FY27 Account Planning Pre-Read — Alaska Airlines
## Prepared for: Client Account Lead | Respondents: 1 | Session: 2026-05-28

---

## 01 · How the client sees us
Alaska Airlines views Accenture as an unknown entity despite acknowledging our contribution at Hawaiian Airlines. CIO Vikram Baskaran sees us as "an impactful advisor who comes at a high cost" but questions whether we can adapt to Alaska's working style. The client perceives us as "bold and too confident" — a poor cultural fit for an organization that "prides themselves in what they have achieved and don't like to recognize their shortcomings." We are viewed as untested and risky during a critical merger transition.

## 02 · Our biggest risk of losing relevance
**Primary risk: Inertia and preference for safe, incremental partnerships.** Alaska favors smaller boutique firms that "land and expand" over large transformation deals. The merger amplifies this risk as leadership prioritizes safe choices over bold moves. Cognizant sits "at the sweet spot with the CIO" precisely because they represent continuity, not disruption. Our existing book of business is at risk of being displaced during integration.

## 03 · What we should stop doing
**Stop pursuing large deals without relationship foundation.** Our sales approach demanding $5M+ commitments upfront is fundamentally misaligned with Alaska's buying behavior. The clear directive: if we're unwilling to start with small strategic deals and build trust incrementally, "this may not be the account for us to pursue." Our current sales strategy actively undermines relationship building.

## 04 · The client relationship
**Critical gap at the top.** We have one primary contact: Vikram Baskaran (CIO), who controls backend systems and most IT decisions. We are completely absent from Charu Jain (CMO), Vikram's peer who owns all customer-facing technology. This represents a 50% coverage gap at the C-level, limiting us to backend IT when front-end digital experience is equally strategic.

## 05 · Where we are underinvested
**Cloud and infrastructure** are our biggest capability gaps. Vikram values quality and impact but sees Cognizant as the safer choice for core infrastructure work. The disconnect: Alaska needs reliability and cost-effectiveness in foundational services, but we're positioned as premium consultants rather than trusted operators.

## 06 · White space
**Two clear opportunities:** IT organization under Vikram (backend) and customer-facing IT under Charu (frontend). **Microsoft** emerges as the ecosystem partner with momentum. The front-end space under Charu represents untapped territory where we have zero presence but significant potential impact.

## 07 · The mega deal
**$50M+ opportunity exists but lacks coherent packaging.** Components identified: Application Management Services, Infrastructure Management Services, AIOps, and CX enablement (AI Governance, change management, API strategy). The opportunity is fragmented across multiple service lines without a unifying business case or delivery approach.

## 08 · Threat board
**Cognizant is the apex predator.** They already deliver IMS, AMS, and all application development for Vikram. Their advantage: proven Alaska relationship, lower cost structure, and incremental approach that feels safe during merger uncertainty. They win by being the known quantity when leadership wants to minimize risk.

## 09 · Our internal obstacle
**Sales strategy misalignment is choking growth.** Our refusal to pursue deals under $5M creates a structural barrier to relationship building. The sales team is "discouraged from supporting relationships" on smaller engagements, preventing the trust-building necessary for larger opportunities. This organizational inflexibility is "our biggest killer" on accounts requiring relationship-first strategies.

## 10 · Bold moves and session priorities
**Bold move:** Propose integrated Cloud Migration + AIOps + IMS/AMS with AI-led SDLC to drive savings, efficiency, and speed while ensuring resilience. **Critical question:** Are we willing to pivot our approach to meet Alaska's preferences, or can we influence them toward more strategic thinking? **Facilitator focus:** How to execute land-and-expand strategy effectively.

## 11 · What this session must do
This session must resolve our fundamental strategic contradiction: we want large deals but refuse to build the small-deal relationships that create large deal opportunities. Success means defining a relationship-first growth strategy with specific small-deal entry points that build toward the $50M+ opportunity. Failure means continuing to pursue transformation deals with a transactional approach, ensuring Cognizant maintains their stranglehold while we remain perpetual outsiders during Alaska's most important transition period.
