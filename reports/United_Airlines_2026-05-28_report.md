# FY27 Account Planning Pre-Read — United Airlines
## Prepared for: Client Account Lead | Respondents: 7 | Session: 2026-05-28

---

## 01 · How the client sees us

United sees us as expensive vendors, not strategic partners. We are perceived as "valuable consulting partner who comes at higher price point than competition" (Basu) and "as a vendor for specific requests" (Hubbard). The Finance domain team believes we're seen as "decent execution partner but we still have more work to do for them to see us as their long term strategic partner" (Schmidt). Knoechel notes we've "made some good impressions... but they do not view us as a go-to partner yet" and questions whether "their culture supports that mentality." Behind closed doors, United likely views us as too expensive and not yet proven as a true partner.

## 02 · Our biggest risk of losing relevance

Two clear risks emerge: **inability to penetrate United Digital/IT** (Basu warns if we "fail to enter United Digital (IT), they would not have a long term annuity revenue") and **macroeconomic pressure** (Nowakowski states "if we cannot operate in a difficult macroeconomic environment for UAL, we will lose them"). Schmidt adds that "external cost pressures could limit internal funding" and notes the CFO/SVP of FP&A "aren't fully bought into the Finance Transformation agenda." The IT penetration risk appears most critical for long-term sustainability.

## 03 · What we should stop doing

**Stop being territorial and overprotective.** Basu is explicit: "Accenture should try to be less protective and territorial and not allow people with subject matter expertise approach client contacts... Our zeal to be perfect and protect client relationships as 'owners' end up costing the opportunities." Nowakowski adds "giving away our work for free." Schmidt notes we need to stop being seen as a "free resource" after "investing quite heavily to remediate a contracting error." Clear consensus: stop internal red tape and stop devaluing our work.

## 04 · The client relationship

**We lack C-suite relationships.** Our contacts are mid-level: Prashant Rao (Procurement), Lindsay Shilney (MD, Procurement COE), Michelle Turner (Director Finance Transformation with "low decision making authority and no budget"). We have some access to Elhan Orhon (Chief Risk Officer) but he's "not actively driving" and has limited influence. **Critical gap: Mike Leskinen (CFO) is not bought in** and "can greenlight everything we want to do in Finance." We're missing Stacey Wronkowski (SD in IT) who "views Accenture as a vendor for an organization that doesn't like consulting vendors." Our influence is constrained to procurement and mid-level finance.

## 05 · Where we are underinvested

**AI/automation and finance optimization** are consistently cited as gaps. We're underinvested in "AI and automation" (Basu, Hubbard, Schmidt) and "Finance and cost optimization" (Nowakowski, Schmidt). Specific gaps include "Application Development (AI led SDLC), Infrastructure and Application Maintenance Services" and senior-level Finance/FP&A relationships. Our biggest contribution appears to be in procurement insights and "kickstarting their Finance Transformation journey."

## 06 · White space

**IMS/AMS represents the biggest opportunity.** Basu identifies "IMS and AMS - big opportunities that will be long term, annuity revenue." He advocates for "Technology Modernization initiatives including SCEPTRE Modernization" and pushing custom AI solutions over SAAS. Schmidt sees "Senior level Finance/FP&A work" as white space. For ecosystem plays, **Oracle has momentum** as "the unofficial preference for their inevitable move to a Cloud ERP" (Schmidt). AWS and IFS also mentioned for SCEPTRE replacement.

## 07 · The mega deal

**No consensus on the $50M+ opportunity.** Three different visions emerge: Basu sees "Implementation partner for project Tango (SCEPTRE replacement)," Nowakowski identifies "Centralizing and modernizing their supply chain," Schmidt proposes "Finance & Supply Chain ERP Transformation," and Hubbard suggests "Reinvention of workforce with the CFO." This divergence indicates we lack a coherent mega-deal strategy.

## 08 · Threat board

**TCS and DXC are most dangerous** because "they are in the system and know most applications inside out... cheaper and come with a more collaborative approach. The people they lead with are technologists and not 'consultants'" (Basu). **Deloitte is actively competing** in Finance transformation. The most insidious threat is **"United internal approach"** (Hubbard) - their default to handle everything internally despite being "slow and unproven." Any competitor offering lower costs with deeper technical expertise poses significant risk.

## 09 · Our internal obstacle

**Sales investment and chargeability pressure conflict with relationship building.** Basu identifies "our inability to invest in Sales people (with Airline experience) to spend time on smaller deals" and "our prejudice that only 'delivery leads' should be talking to the client." Hubbard calls out "balancing chargeability/sales targets with building relationships with very little ROI." Schmidt notes we need to "somehow... incentivize" better engagement. Our operating model change is creating internal misalignment.

## 10 · Bold moves and session priorities

**Bold move consensus: Direct C-suite engagement.** Basu wants to "approach key stakeholders in United Digital (IT) on our approach to Agentic AI" while Schmidt seeks to "solidify a meeting with the CFO." Nowakowski asks "Where can we help them take cost out now?" **The session must answer:** "Is United a long term strategic account that leadership is willing to invest time/resources in for the next couple years?" (Schmidt) and "Do we really want to be aggressively going to our client or are we going to stay course?" (Basu).

## 11 · What this session must do

This session must decide whether we're willing to make sustained investment in United despite current low returns, or if we'll continue opportunistic engagement. If we choose investment, we must align on a single mega-deal pursuit strategy and commit resources to penetrate C-suite relationships, particularly the CFO and United Digital leadership. Success means walking out with clear investment commitments and unified account strategy. Failure means continuing our current expensive, fragmented vendor status with diminishing returns.
