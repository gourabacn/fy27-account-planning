# FY27 Account Planning Pre-Read — Alaska Airlines
## Prepared for: Client Account Lead | Respondents: 1 | Session: 2026-05-22

---

## 01 · How the client sees us
Alaska Airlines acknowledges our Hawaiian Airlines success but views us as unproven at their organization. CIO Vikram Baskaran sees Accenture as "an impactful advisor who comes at a high cost" and recognizes our value and quality. However, the client perceives us as "bold and too confident" for an organization that "prides themselves in what they have achieved and don't like to recognize their shortcomings." They're skeptical about our ability to adapt to Alaska's ways of working.

## 02 · Our biggest risk of losing relevance
**Inertia is the primary threat.** Alaska operates with an "order taker mindset" and prefers buying from incumbent preferred providers (IPPs). Cognizant sits at the "sweet spot with the CIO" and represents the safe, cheap option during this critical merger transition. The client favors smaller boutique firms with land-and-expand approaches over large deal constructs requiring transformation. With the merger, we risk losing existing book of business entirely.

## 03 · What we should stop doing
Stop insisting on large strategic deals as entry points. Our unwillingness to work small strategic deals to build trust is counterproductive. The respondent warns: "If we are not willing to work on small strategic deals and build the trust with smaller asks in the IT organization and be ready to land and expand, this may not be the account for us to pursue."

## 04 · The client relationship
**Critical relationship gap exists.** We have connection to Vikram Baskaran (CIO, primary decision maker for backend IT systems) but are missing Charu Jain (CMO, Vikram's peer who owns all front-end customer-facing IT). This represents a fundamental blind spot—we're only covering half the technology organization during a merger integration.

## 05 · Where we are underinvested
Most important client need: Cloud migration and infrastructure management during merger transition. Biggest gaps: Cloud and infrastructure capabilities, plus relationship coverage with the CMO organization. We're underinvested in the very areas the client needs most help with during this critical juncture.

## 06 · White space
Two clear opportunities: IT organization under Vikram (backend systems) and customer-facing IT under Charu (front-end). Microsoft partnership presents the strongest ecosystem play. The merger creates urgency around both infrastructure consolidation and customer experience integration.

## 07 · The mega deal
Coherent $50M+ vision exists: Application Management Services + Infrastructure Management Services + AIOps + CX enablement (AI Governance, Change management post-PSS, API usage). However, this requires land-and-expand approach starting with smaller engagements to build trust and prove value.

## 08 · Threat board
**Cognizant is the most dangerous competitor.** They currently handle IMS, AMS, and all application development work for Vikram. Client sees them as "safe and cheap" during the merger transition. Vikram fears "putting all eggs in one basket" with a firm he hasn't worked extensively with, especially when his position could be jeopardized by transition failures.

## 09 · Our internal obstacle
**Sales strategy misalignment.** Our sales team only focuses on projects >$5M, creating a fundamental mismatch with this client's preference for starting small and expanding. The respondent is direct: "Our inability to be flexible and discouraging sales professionals from supporting with relationships will be our biggest killer."

## 10 · Bold moves and session priorities
**Bold move:** Approach Vikram with integrated Cloud Migration + AIOps + IMS/AMS offering combined with AI-led SDLC to drive savings, efficiency, speed-to-market, and resilience. **Session question:** Are we ready to pivot to meet the client's working style or influence them toward more strategic thinking? **Facilitator focus:** Land-and-expand strategy development.

## 11 · What this session must do
This session must resolve the fundamental strategic tension between our large-deal sales approach and Alaska's small-deal entry preference. Success means defining a credible land-and-expand strategy that sales will execute, starting with sub-$5M engagements that build toward the mega-deal vision. Failure means continuing to pursue large deals that will never materialize while Cognizant solidifies their position during the merger integration.
