# FY27 Account Planning Pre-Read — Alaska Airlines
## Prepared for: Client Account Lead | Respondents: 1 | Session: 2026-05-22

---

## 01 · How the client sees us
Alaska Airlines acknowledges Accenture's contribution at Hawaiian Airlines but views us as unproven at Alaska specifically. CIO Vikram Baskaran sees us as an "impactful advisor who comes at a high cost"—he recognizes our value and quality but considers us risky. The client perceives us as "bold and too confident" while they pride themselves on their achievements and resist recognizing shortcomings. Behind closed doors, they likely see us as expensive consultants who haven't earned our stripes at Alaska and may disrupt their comfortable status quo.

## 02 · Our biggest risk of losing relevance
**Inertia is the #1 risk.** Alaska traditionally buys from IPPs with an "order taker mindset" and favors smaller boutique firms that land and expand over large deal constructs requiring transformation. Cognizant sits in the "sweet spot" with the CIO, positioning as safe and cheap. The merger compounds this risk—we may lose existing book of business while failing to penetrate Alaska's preference for incremental, trusted partnerships over bold transformation plays.

## 03 · What we should stop doing
**Stop pursuing only large deals ($5M+) and abandoning relationship-building on smaller strategic engagements.** The response is unequivocal: if we're unwilling to work small strategic deals to build trust, "this may not be the account for us to pursue." Our sales approach of dropping focus when deals don't reach $10M+ commitments will kill relationships before they develop. There is clear consensus (from the single respondent) that our inflexibility on deal size is self-defeating.

## 04 · The client relationship
**We know one person. We're missing the other decision-maker.** Our primary contact is Vikram Baskaran (CIO), who controls backend systems and most IT initiatives. Critical gap: Charu Jain (CMO), Vikram's peer who owns all front-end customer-facing IT. This 50% coverage of key decision-makers severely limits our influence across the technology organization and blocks access to customer experience opportunities.

## 05 · Where we are underinvested
**Underinvested: Cloud and infrastructure.** This directly conflicts with where we should be making our "most important contribution"—the response identifies cloud migration and infrastructure as both our biggest gap and our biggest opportunity. The misalignment is stark: we're weak where we need to be strong, particularly as the client sees us as expensive but hasn't experienced our core cloud capabilities.

## 06 · White space
**Two clear territories: IT org under Vikram and customer-facing IT under Charu.** The white space maps directly to our relationship gaps—we're missing half the opportunity. **Microsoft ecosystem partnership** has momentum, suggesting a path to relevance through established client relationships and technology investments.

## 07 · The mega deal
**Coherent $50M+ opportunity exists: Application Management Services + Infrastructure Management Services + AIOps + CX enablement** (AI Governance, Change management, API usage). This represents a full-stack play combining our underinvested areas with emerging AI capabilities. However, there's no indication of client appetite for this scale—the mega deal vision contradicts the client's preference for smaller, incremental engagements.

## 08 · Threat board
**Cognizant is the most dangerous competitor.** They already do IMS, AMS, and all app development work for Vikram. They're positioned as "safe and cheap" during a critical transition period. The client would choose them because they're low-risk, proven, and aligned with Alaska's preference for incremental partnerships over transformation. They own the incumbent advantage during a merger when stability trumps innovation.

## 09 · Our internal obstacle
**Sales team inflexibility on deal size is broken.** Sales professionals are discouraged from supporting relationships on deals under $5M, despite client preference for smaller strategic engagements that build trust. This structural misalignment between our sales approach and client buying behavior is identified as "our biggest killer." The obstacle is behavioral and organizational, not capability-based.

## 10 · Bold moves and session priorities
**Bold move:** Approach Vikram with integrated Cloud Migration + AIOps + IMS/AMS offering, positioning AI-led SDLC to drive savings, efficiency, speed-to-market, and resilience. **Critical question:** Are we ready to pivot our approach to meet client preferences or influence them toward strategic thinking? **Facilitator focus:** How to land and expand effectively.

## 11 · What this session must do
**This session must resolve the fundamental tension between our large-deal sales model and Alaska's small-deal buying preference.** Success means defining a land-and-expand strategy that builds trust through smaller strategic wins while positioning for larger transformation opportunities. Failure means continuing to pitch mega-deals to a client that wants incremental partnerships, ensuring Cognizant maintains their incumbent advantage while we remain expensive and irrelevant. The session must answer whether we adapt to Alaska or walk away.
